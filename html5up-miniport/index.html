#Tutorial: Creating Color Swatch Components in Figma



In Figma, creating color swatch components is an essential step in building a design system. These components allow you to organize and document your color palette effectively, making it easy to maintain consistency throughout your design projects. Here's how to create color swatch components step by step:


Step 1: Open Your Figma Project Ensure you have your design project open in Figma, or create a new one if needed. This tutorial assumes you've already set up some colors that you want to organize into swatch components.

Step 2: Select the Colors You Want to Include In your Figma project, select the shapes, text, or elements that represent the colors you want to turn into swatch components. You can do this by clicking on each color individually while holding down the Shift key to select multiple colors.

Step 3: Create Components With your colors selected, right-click on one of them, and from the context menu, choose "Create Component." Alternatively, you can use the keyboard shortcut `Ctrl` + `Alt` + `K` (Windows) or `Cmd` + `Option` + `K` (Mac).

Step 4: Name Your Component A dialog box will appear for naming your component. Give it a descriptive name that represents the color, such as "Primary Blue" or "Accent Red." This name will help you identify the color easily in the future.

Step 5: Add the Component to a Frame After naming your component, you'll notice it in the Assets panel on the left side of the Figma interface. Click and drag the color swatch component into a frame or section dedicated to organizing your color palette. This frame will serve as your color palette reference.

Step 6: Duplicate and Arrange Swatches To create additional swatches, duplicate the color swatch component you just created by right-clicking on it in the Assets panel and selecting "Duplicate." You can create as many swatches as you need for your color palette. Arrange the swatches within the frame to visualize your color palette effectively. You can use grid or alignment tools to maintain consistency.

Step 7: Edit and Update Swatches Whenever you need to update a color in your palette, double-click on the color swatch component in the Assets panel. Make your color adjustments, and these changes will automatically reflect wherever you've used that color in your design.

Step 8: Apply Swatches to Your Designs To use the color swatch components in your designs, simply drag and drop them onto shapes or text elements. This ensures that your design remains consistent, and any updates to the color palette are applied globally.

Step 9: Share Your Design System If you're working on a collaborative project, be sure to share your design system with your team by publishing the components and styles. This ensures everyone is using the same colors and maintains design consistency.

By following these steps, you can easily create and manage color swatch components in Figma, helping you maintain a cohesive and organized color palette within your design system.

Topics Mentioned in the Reading:


Constructive Feedback:

Constructive feedback refers to providing feedback that is aimed at helping individuals improve their work or behavior. It focuses on pointing out areas for improvement while offering guidance and support.

Reinforcing Feedback:

Reinforcing feedback is feedback that encourages individuals to continue positive behaviors. It acknowledges and highlights behaviors or actions that are beneficial and should be continued.

Redirecting Feedback:

Redirecting feedback is given when there is a need for individuals to change specific behaviors. It involves pointing out what needs to stop or be altered and suggesting an alternative course of action.

Feedback Culture:

Feedback culture refers to the environment within an organization or team where feedback is not only accepted but actively encouraged. It involves creating a space where open and honest feedback is valued and integrated into daily processes.

The artwork I've chosen for analysis is "New York, Night," an oil painting created by the renowned American artist Georgia O'Keeffe in 1928-29. This masterpiece is a striking portrayal of New York City at night. In this blog post, we will delve into the history of this artwork and explore three critical areas of analysis: "abstraction," "visual composition," and "emotional resonance."


Abstraction: Georgia O'Keeffe is well-known for her abstract and highly stylized approach to art, and "New York, Night" is no exception. In this painting, O'Keeffe takes the recognizable skyline of New York City and simplifies it into bold, geometric forms. The skyscrapers are reduced to elongated, minimalist shapes, emphasizing their towering presence. This abstraction serves to distill the essence of the cityscape, allowing viewers to focus on its architectural grandeur while stripping away unnecessary details. O'Keeffe's abstract interpretation challenges viewers to see the city from a fresh perspective.

Visual Composition: The composition of "New York, Night" is a masterpiece of balance and contrast. O'Keeffe's use of vertical lines for the skyscrapers and the incorporation of negative space between them create a sense of rhythm and harmony. The painting's tall, slender format further accentuates the verticality of the city's buildings. The contrast between the deep, dark blues and the radiant, golden-yellow highlights, down to the red light reflection over the bottom left buildings, evokes a vivid sense of the night sky illuminated by the city's lights. O'Keeffe's careful visual composition draws the viewer into the nocturnal ambiance of New York City.

Emotional Resonance: "New York, Night" exudes a sense of quietude and contemplation, an emotional resonance that sets it apart from traditional cityscape paintings. O'Keeffe's portrayal of the city at night captures a tranquil, almost meditative atmosphere, in stark contrast to the city's usual bustling energy. The use of deep blues and subdued yellows imparts a sense of serenity, inviting viewers to reflect on the city's dual nature—its frenetic pace during the day and its quiet moments under the night sky. O'Keeffe's ability to evoke this emotional response through her abstract representation is a testament to her artistic prowess.

UIUN Summit: Exploring Inclusivity in UI Design



Introduction:

The UIUN Summit was not your typical class exercise but a transformative Accessibility Workshop intended to stimulate young designers' thinking about UI accessibility from a new perspective. It functioned as a mock United Nations of UI Accessibility, where each team represented a specific population and proposed necessary changes for the Sarpy County election commission site's mail-in ballot interface.

Highlights of My Population:

My partner and I represented "Children Under 13," a unique and vibrant group. We explored how this age group experiences the world, emphasizing their critical stage of cognitive and motor skill development, limited reading abilities, and strong visual comprehension. The needs of children under 13 included simplicity, flexibility, visually appealing and interactive elements, and minimal text content with clear instructions. Our original recommendations were geared toward these needs.

The Negotiations:

The negotiations during the summit were surprisingly effective and understanding. Teams were open to listening and collaborating. It was a collective effort to find common ground and create a comprehensive set of requirements that could meet the needs of multiple populations.

What I Fought For:

I advocated for inclusivity. We aimed to ensure that our recommendations weren't exclusive to just children under 13 but could benefit other populations as well. For example, we pushed for clear and inclusive icons, autocorrect features, and visually appealing interfaces, which could also aid non-native speakers and individuals with intellectual disabilities.

Changing Requirements:

Our requirements evolved significantly during the negotiations. We joined forces with other groups to add more enhancement features that were inclusive of multiple populations. It was enlightening to see how collaborative efforts could lead to more comprehensive solutions.

Unlikely Allies:

Our unlikely allies turned out to be the group representing non-native speakers (ESL). We resonated with each other's ideas, particularly regarding the importance of clear, inclusive icons and intuitive design. We shared the common goal of making the interface understandable to a broader audience. Conversely, the group representing Gen Z was the hardest to convince. They prioritized speed over interactive feedback, which was different from our focus on child-friendly and interactive elements.

Prioritizing Needs:

Prioritizing the needs of different user populations during the UIUN Summit was a challenging but necessary task. We had to find a balance between various requirements. For instance, we emphasized clarity and simplicity in design for some populations, focusing on making the interface user-friendly and straightforward. On the other hand, for children under 13, our priority was on gamification and limited text content, aiming to engage them with interactive elements and child-friendly visuals. This experience taught us the importance of compromise and the significance of finding a middle ground to meet the diverse needs of the user groups.

Greatest Takeaways:

The UIUN Summit was a unique experience that provided valuable insights into the complexities of UI accessibility. It emphasized the importance of collaboration and inclusivity in design, showcasing how designing with a specific population in mind can have far-reaching benefits. It also revealed that the strategies for UI accessibility are not exclusive to one group; they can be applied to benefit a wide range of users. These lessons will undoubtedly be applied to other topics and projects, ensuring that accessibility remains a central focus in my future endeavors.

Design Evolution Narrative

Role in the Project:

Collaborating closely with my partner, we initiated the project by envisioning a package tracking solution tailored for creative professionals such as graphic designers. As the primary designer, my focus centered on refining the visual aesthetics and ensuring a seamless user experience. We also divided our user interviews to get unique feedback from different perspectives. We believed this would make our design richer due to the multi-angled view points that would contribute to diverse design ideas. My partner’s interviews and personal experience of living in an apartment, in which deliveries are more often delivered to the wrong tenant, were mostly focussed on the purpose of our application and the motivation behind its initiation to improve lives by providing a solution. Since I dealt with most of the mockup designs, I aimed to address the needs of my interviewees by incorporating solutions within my design, which encompassed the issues faced by my partner's interviews as we thoroughly communicated.

Inspiration and Target Audience:

Our inspiration was drawn from crafted user scenarios, exemplified by Emma, the graphic designer, and Michael, the stay-at-home parent. Their challenges with managing multiple deliveries resonated with us, sparking the idea to create an app that caters to the specific needs of busy individuals. Personally invested in the project, I saw the potential to streamline the lives of creative professionals through thoughtful design. Understanding Emma's need for efficiency and Michael's desire for simplicity, we integrated two key features into our app: real-time delivery status updates and a simple UI that consolidates all deliveries in one place. Our target audience, comprising graphic designers and stay-at-home parents, faces the common challenge of multitasking and staying organized. The objective was to provide a user-friendly package tracking app, alleviating stress, enhancing efficiency, and bringing ease to their daily routines. Inspired by Emma's desire to track the exact location of her art supplies in real-time, we implemented a feature that provides instant updates on the status of each delivery. This real-time tracking capability ensures that users like Emma can stay informed, even in the midst of a busy schedule, allowing them to plan their activities around the expected delivery time. Furthermore, drawing inspiration from Michael's struggle to keep track of multiple delivery schedules, we crafted a user interface that simplifies the tracking process. All deliveries, regardless of the vendor, are displayed in one place with clear and concise information. This user-friendly design ensures that individuals like Michael can easily manage their household responsibilities while staying on top of their online orders.

Target Device Type:

Initially conceptualized as a web application, we transitioned to a mobile app during our design process with Figma. Recognizing the on-the-go nature of our target users, a mobile app proved more accessible and convenient, facilitating tracking wherever our users are. Considering Emma's busy studio environment and Michael's juggling act as a stay-at-home parent, the app facilitates effortless planning. Users can quickly check the delivery status on the mobile app during breaks or while multitasking, empowering them to make informed decisions about their daily activities in a heartbeat!

Story-Inspired Design:

The narrative of Emma checking her art supply deliveries during a hectic workday and Michael managing deliveries amidst family activities guided our design choices. This narrative-driven approach influenced the creation of a streamlined mobile friendly navigation bar, a straightforward orders listing page, and real-time tracking updates for effective planning.

Early Sketches and Figma Mockup:

Our journey began with early sketches envisioning key components. Initially planned for a web platform (as previously mentioned), we later adapted our design to the mobile app format using Figma, which allowed for a more accessible and user-friendly experience. The Figma mockup encapsulated the flow and visual elements we envisioned with the values of a clear, intuitive UI and an efficient design where everything is organized in a user-centric manner.

Design System Choices:

Carefully selecting a color palette balancing creativity with clarity, we aimed for rounded corners on buttons and inputs to convey a modern and approachable feel. The minimalist design ensured a clean and focused user interface, catering to the mobile app format. We wanted to esure intuitiveness and clarity, hence we aimed for the home page to contain all the incoming orders. We also wanted the design to be efficient for the user, hence we provided navigation icons where the user can access the main pages like, account info, settings, and home without a hassle. We chose the color dark blue as it motivates the feeling of trust and formality, and we paired it with high contrast colors like white and light gray to be inclusive of all users that may potentially have visual impairments. We ensured our buttons were large enough for users suffering from hand tremors or Parkinson’s like conditions. We also chose universally assumed icons for any users who may have trouble reading labels. We avoided using other bright colors like red to not cause an overflow or bleed of different color schemes, which may cause discomfort to the user’s vision.

We really wanted our design to fulfill the values of clarity and efficiency for a diverse user base, hence we enhanced our accessibility with these changes. This would be much more efficient for users to navigate the different pages rather than having to press the back barrow multiple times to reach the main orders page. Regarding the tracking input, it was as simple as locking the frame in place on the Figma prototype. Our logo of choice is a squiggle with two location pins symbolizing the route followed by the delivery personnel!

User Testing and Feedback:

Conducting user testing involved the input of various perspectives of diverse backgrounds, including graphic designers, parents, and tech-savvy students at UNL. We envisioned these interactions, noting confusion points, and collected valuable feedback from these scenarios. We wanted to ensure our design was inclusive of all populations, and valuable to as many users as it can be. Our user testers were composed of other peers in University and we took feedback from about 4-5 different students, a graphic designer, and a college professor. For each user, our debrief presented our motivation to provide a clearly intuitive and efficient solution, referencing our design values, for delivery tracking. The task for each user was to login, view the main page, view a specific order, navigate to customer care, followed by the accessing settings page then returning home to the main orders. User feedback prioritized issues such as text visibility, icon intuitiveness, and theme clarity. Adjustments included refining text color for high contrast visibility to all users, adding intuitive labeling to icons for clarity, adding a home button in the settings page, fixing confusing prototype glitches like the non-appearing tracking input, and optimizing the theme colors/contrast for better visibility.

Understanding this analogy is crucial for career success, emphasizing the importance of strategic planning, effective communication, and the ability to anticipate and address challenges in the dynamic field of UX design.
